Study Cases

This page contains description of three study cases of semi-supervised peak calling approach.

  • CD14+ ENCODE Data
  • ULI-ChIP-Seq Data
  • McGill Data

CD14+ ENCODE Data

To ensure that the quality of SPAN peak calling does not stem from introducing computational artifacts, we compared the peaks called from our samples to the ones identified in the ENCODE project for the CD14+ monocytes. Namely, we calculated the fraction of peaks for each of our samples that overlapped with the corresponding ENCODE sample, and vice versa, the fraction of peaks from the ENCODE sample that overlapped with the peaks of each of our samples (since these are not symmetric characteristics).
Peaks called by SPAN consistently overlapped with ~80% of ENCODE peaks in both directions, while traditional peak calling approaches resulted in significant disparities (caused in part by the variability in the number of peaks called for each sample).

ULI-ChIP-seq Data

As a benchmark we decided to apply SPAN peak calling approach to another ULI-ChIP-seq dataset.

Chen, C. et al. presented an ultra-low-input micrococcal nuclease-based native ChIP (ULI-NChIP) and sequencing method to generate genome-wide histone mark profiles with high resolution and reproducibility from as few as one thousand cells. They generated data for both broad and narrow histone marks from 103-106 cells. Among others in GSE63523 there are H3K4me3 tracks for 5,000, 10,000 and 100,000 cells and H3K27me3 for 1,000, 10,000 and 100,000 cells. We used these tracks to assess the semi-supervised approach in extreme conditions. Raw reads were processed using technical pipeline described in the section Processing. The only difference was in paired end sequencing and alignment. Also, we used MACS2 with FDR=0.05 and FDR=1E-6.

SPAN pipeline yielded ~10,000 H3K4me3 peaks and ~20,000 H3K27me3 peaks for 100,000 cells, which is comparable with ENCODE and aging study peaks. SPAN produced approximately the same number of peaks for H3K27me3 for 10,000 and 100,000 cells (~20,000) and half of the peaks for 1,000 cells, while MACS2 with relaxed FDR produces too many peaks in all cases and too few peaks for 10,000 cells (~7,000 peaks). Situation is even worse for H3K4me3 modification, as MACS2 produced too many peaks for relaxed and too few for stringent FDR control, while SPAN produced ~17,000 peaks, which is comparable to what we see in other study cases and the project data.

All the peak callers failed given fewer than 100,000 cells for H3K4me3, which suggests that this is a minimum required for high-quality peak calling.

McGill Data

SPAN tuning mode was inspired by the article by Hocking, T. D. et al., in which authors investigated the benefits of tuning approach for golden standard peak callers. The authors used the McGill Epigenomics Mapping Centre data (McGill University, Montr√©al, Canada).
The Hocking et al. experimental dataset consisted of 37 human cell samples. T cells, B cells and monocytes were grouped as "immune" (27 samples), while kidney, skeletal muscle and leukemic B cells were grouped as "other" (10 samples). Each sample was subjected to H3K4me3-specific ChIP-seq procedure, and 29 samples were additionally subjected to H3K36me3-specific procedure (21 immune and 8 other).
A set of cell-type specific labels was created for each dataset. Each label belongs to one of the same four types that we use for semi-supervised SPAN peak calling (peaks, noPeaks, peakStart, peakEnd). The peak caller settings that optimized the total dataset error were determined, and the peaks obtained with those settings were compared to the peaks produced by the caller with the default settings.

Our proposed tuning method differs from original one: in our case, tuning is performed for each track individually, meaning we assign an optimal parameter value to each track in each dataset, while the McGill experiment selects a single one that optimizes the total dataset error. SPAN approach offers tangible benefits, since we have empirically confirmed that different quality tracks require different FDR cutoffs.
Nevertheless, we have also investigated SPAN in the original way and achieved remarkably good results: SPAN performed comparable with MACS2 on H3K4me3 and significantly outperformed both MACS2 and SICER on wide modification H3K36me3.

We have also used the McGill data to investigate how much do the labels used for tuning influence the called peaks. We selected two cell types, specifically monocytes and T cells, which we expected to have fairly different H3K4me3 enrichment profiles. We selected 11 good quality (top signal-to-noise ratio) H3K4me3 tracks for these cell types from the McGill data (five monocyte tracks and six T cell tracks). We've generated a single set of labels applicable to all tracks by labeling the transcription start sites of some of the housekeeping genes. As expected, these TSSes were enriched in all tracks. To produce the negative control noPeaks labels, we used the body of the same genes, since, as expected, these gene bodies were not enriched in H3K4me3 in any of the tracks. The label set consisting of 44 labels is available for download. Eleven selected tracks were tuned individually using this label set.
We expected to see that monocyte peaks would be observably different from the T cell peaks, despite having been tuned on the exact same label set, while different monocyte tracks would be similar, as well as different T cell tracks. The results confirmed our expectations readily.
SPAN was able to produce reasonable number of peaks for H3K4me3 for both cell types, comparable to aging study data. Thus, we confirmed our expectations that cell type differences are be preserved even when the label set is the same for all tracks.

Summary

SPAN Peak Analyzer can be used as a general purpose peak calling solution, applicable to any type of ChIP-Seq experiments.
